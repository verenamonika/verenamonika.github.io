About me

I am an Austrian PhD student at the Max Planck Institute for Astronomy in Heidelberg (Germany) as part of the IMPRS-HD program. I work on the dispersal of open clusters, stellar associations and streams in the Milky Way disk, using astrometric and photometric data provided by the Gaia mission. Before this, I did my Bachelor and Master in the Department of Astrophysics at the University of Vienna (Austria).

Research

Young stars are clustered across a wide range of scales, from compact bound objects to ‘streams‘ that extend over several hundred parsecs. Using data provided by Gaia, we study the orbit space clustering of stars in the extended solar neighbourhood. Some of these orbit clumps are birth remnants of clusters, associations and stellar streams, others reflect stars herded into particular orbits as a consequence of dynamical resonances. The aim of this project is to address and answer the following questions: What is the origin of the substructure in the orbit distribution? On what orbits are stars born? How clumpy is the distribution of birth places of stars? At what rate do young clusters disperse and what does this tell us about the transition to the Galactic field population? Selected Publications

*ongoing* Likelihood isochrone fitting

Fürnkranz, Rix, Coronado & Seeburger 2023 (accepted)

We have developed a method to derive ages of stellar action-angle groups: This requires projecting their action-angle distribution into 5D parallax-proper motion-position space and drawing CMDs from the entire Gaia catalog. We then perform a likelihood isochrone fitting, which accounts for widely varying distances and reddenings, outliers and binaries, sparsely populated main sequence turn-offs, and the possible presence of an intrinsic age spread in the stellar population.

'Pearls on a string'

ADS: Coronado, Fürnkranz & Rix 2022

We have identified algorithmically the most prominent ensembles of stars in the ~1kpc around the Sun that are clustered in orbit and orbital phase space, or action-angle space. Some of these are established clusters, but many are unrecognized associations or streams. We discovered that different clusters and associations are frequently found on very similar orbits, merely at different orbital phases, like 'pearls on a string'.

120° stellar stream

ADS: Meingast, Alves & Fürnkranz 2019

Using the 6D position-velocity information provided by Gaia DR2, we discovered the closest coeval stellar stream so far.

Coma Berenices tidal tails

ADS: Fürnkranz, Meingast & Alves 2019

We unveiled the long predicted tidal tails of the open cluster Coma Berenices. Furthermore, we discovered a second group of stars, located in the vicinity of Coma Berenices. Orbit integrations showed that both groups are going to overlap in the near future.
