About me

I am an Austrian PhD student at the Max Planck Institute for Astronomy in Heidelberg (Germany), working on the dispersal of open clusters, stellar associations and streams in the Milky Way disk. For my research, I use astrometric and photometric data provided by the Gaia mission. Before this, I did my Bachelor and Master in the Department of Astrophysics at the University of Vienna (Austria).

We find stellar groups frequently on similar orbits, merely at different orbital phases

Stars originate from the dense interstellar medium, which exhibits filamentary structure to scales of $\sim 1$~kpc in galaxies like our Milky Way. Consequently, stars are not born at random but typically in stellar clusters or associations, where distinct star clusters can be on uncannily similar orbits. In this paper we explore quantitatively how much large-scale correlation there is among different stellar clusters and associations in \emph{orbit phase space}, characterized here by actions and angles. As a starting point, we identified 55 prominent stellar overdensities in the 6D space of both orbit (three actions) and orbital phase (three angles), among the $\sim$ 2.8 million stars within $d \leq 800$~pc with radial velocities from Gaia EDR3. We then explored the orbital \emph{phase} distribution of all sample stars in the same \emph{orbit} patch as any one of these 55 overdensities, i.e. considered all stars with nearly the same orbital actions, irrespective of orbital phase. We find that very commonly numerous additional distinct orbital phase overdensities exist along these same orbits, like pearls on a string. These `pearls' range from known stellar clusters to loose, unrecognized associations. Among orbit patches defined by one initial orbit-phase overdensity 50\% contain at least 8 additional orbital-phase pearls of 10 . Read more: Coronado, Fürnkranz & Rix, ApJ, 2022
